## 4) Criterion Detail Pages

### 4.1 Target Relevance
Score: **3 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E4_asset_specific_clinical_evidence**  
Main line: **KCNT1 gain-of-function epilepsy is a direct SKBP E/I Balance / Ion Channel fit, and ABS-1230 is clinically positioned against the genetically defined causal target.**

What was checked:
- Target identity: KCNT1 / Slack sodium-activated potassium channel
- Disease/biology relevance: pathogenic KCNT1 variants increase channel activity and are linked to severe early-onset epilepsy
- SKBP Theme / Cluster fit: E/I Balance / Ion Channel
- General epilepsy relevance: severe treatment-resistant pediatric developmental and epileptic encephalopathy

Evidence trail:
- Actio pipeline identifies ABS-1230 as an oral small molecule KCNT1 inhibitor for KCNT1-related epilepsy and lists FDA Fast Track, Rare Pediatric Disease, Orphan Drug, and RDEP acceptance signals. [4], [7]
- CHOP describes KCNT1 as encoding a brain potassium channel and notes pathogenic variants typically increase channel activity, presumed to cause epilepsy by altering neuronal electrical activity. [10]
- Actio’s KYRON trial page describes ABS-1230 as targeting overactive KCNT1 ion channel overactivity to reduce seizures. [8]
- ClinicalTrials.gov lists the Phase 1b/2 trial in KCNT1-related epilepsy. [11]

Investigation note:
- Score 3 is appropriate because target, disease, causal biology, modality, and clinical indication align tightly with SKBP E/I Balance / Ion Channel logic.
- Why not higher: already maximum score.
- Uncertain points: broader relevance beyond KCNT1-related epilepsy into other genetic epilepsies remains unproven clinically.

### 4.2 Competitive Landscape
Score: **2 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E4_asset_specific_clinical_evidence**  
Main line: **ABS-1230 has a credible first-in-class oral small molecule position, but same-target clinical and preclinical competitors are already visible.**

What was checked:
- Same disease competitors
- Same target competitors
- Same or similar MoA competitors
- Front runner count
- Approved / Phase 3 / clinical / preclinical status

Competitor table:

| Competitor | Company | Modality | Target / MoA | Stage | Why it matters | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| S230815 / KANDLE Study | Servier | Intrathecal ASO, per KCNT1 Foundation; official trial describes intrathecal S230815 | KCNT1 activity reduction / blocking KCNT1 activity | Phase 1b/2 | Direct same-target, same-disease clinical competitor; less convenient delivery than oral ABS-1230 but potentially disease-modifying gene-level approach | [13], [14] |
| ATL-201 | Atalanta Therapeutics | di-siRNA / RNAi | KCNT1 transcript reduction / neuronal excitability normalization | Preclinical; IND submission planned in 2025 per company | Direct same-target genetic epilepsy competitor with durable seizure suppression in mouse model; CNS oligonucleotide delivery could compete if clinical translation succeeds | [15], [16] |
| Quinidine | Generic / off-label | Oral small molecule antiarrhythmic | Partial KCNT1 channel blocker | Off-label / not approved for KCNT1 | Historical same-target pharmacology; mixed efficacy and cardiotoxicity create unmet need for selective KCNT1 inhibitors | [17], [18] |
| Bexicaserin / LP352 | Lundbeck / Longboard | Oral small molecule | 5-HT2C superagonist; not KCNT1 | Phase 3 DEEp Program | Broader DEE seizure competitor; advanced stage could shape payer/clinical expectations but not disease-causal for KCNT1 | [19], [20] |
| FINTEPLA / fenfluramine | UCB | Oral small molecule | Serotonergic antiseizure mechanism; not KCNT1 | Approved for Dravet syndrome and Lennox-Gastaut syndrome | Approved rare epilepsy comparator; demonstrates payer acceptance and seizure-reduction benchmark but not KCNT1-targeted | [21], [22] |
| ZTALMY / ganaxolone | Marinus / Immedica | Oral neuroactive steroid | GABA-A receptor positive allosteric modulator | Approved for CDKL5 deficiency disorder seizures | Approved genetic epilepsy comparator; broad symptomatic antiseizure approach rather than causal KCNT1 modulation | [23] |

Investigation note:
- The competitive search started with same disease / same target and then broadened to DEE and rare genetic epilepsy assets.
- True same-MoA / same-target front runners are **S230815** and **ATL-201**; broader seizure competitors such as bexicaserin, FINTEPLA, and ZTALMY are relevant mainly for clinical and commercial benchmarking.
- Score 2 is selected because there is no approved KCNT1-targeted drug, but direct competitors are active and could compress share.
- Why not higher: not a clean white-space category; at least one same-target Phase 1b/2 program exists.
- Uncertain points: S230815’s exact public modality details are clearer through KCNT1 Foundation as ASO; official Servier trial page confirms intrathecal S230815 and KCNT1 activity blocking but does not prominently describe ASO chemistry.

### 4.3 MoA Validity
Score: **3 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E3_asset_specific_preclinical_or_technical_evidence**  
Main line: **ABS-1230 has strong asset-specific mechanistic evidence: potent KCNT1 inhibition, broad pathogenic mutant coverage, high selectivity, CNS penetration, and seizure reduction in KCNT1 mutant mice.**

What was checked:
- Journal publication / PMID / DOI
- Mechanistic consistency
- Functional readout
- Disease linkage
- Safety-relevant signal

Evidence trail:
- AES 2025 poster reports ABS-1230 inhibited human and mouse KCNT1-dependent K+ current with IC50 values of 0.25 and 0.12 nM. [9]
- The same poster reports potent inhibition of 63 pathogenic or likely pathogenic mutants with IC50 values of 0.8–8.8 nM. [9]
- The poster reports no meaningful activity against >500 off-target proteins examined and no significant activity against other proteins at 1 µM except KCNT1. [9]
- ABS-1230 demonstrated high oral bioavailability and brain penetration across species and dose-dependent seizure reduction in Kcnt1R409Q/R409Q mice. [9]
- Company press release states preclinical evidence supports Phase 1a and planned Phase 1b/2 progression. [7]

Investigation note:
- Score 3 is justified by quantitative, asset-specific functional and in vivo data that map directly to KCNT1 gain-of-function disease biology.
- Why not higher: already maximum score; however, clinical seizure reduction in patients has not yet been shown.
- Uncertain points: data are currently from company/AES poster rather than peer-reviewed full manuscript; human pharmacodynamic proof is not yet public.

### 4.4 Platform Attractiveness
Score: **2 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E3_asset_specific_preclinical_or_technical_evidence**  
Main line: **Oral CNS small molecule modality is highly attractive versus intrathecal oligonucleotide competitors, but the broader platform is less independently validated than the asset-level pharmacology.**

What was checked:
- Is the platform real and reproducible?
- Is differentiation supported by data?
- Does modality fit SKBP priorities?
- Preferred modalities: small molecule, ASO, siRNA
- Secondary modalities: AOC, antibody, biologic

Evidence trail:
- Actio describes a genetics-driven approach using target identification, molecule design, mutation characterization, and indication expansion. [5]
- ABS-1230 is an oral small molecule intended to inhibit all disease-causing variants of KCNT1. [4], [7], [9]
- Asset-specific poster supports oral bioavailability, brain penetration, selectivity, and broad mutant inhibition. [9]
- Company pipeline also includes ABS-0871, another oral small molecule ion-channel program, suggesting repeatability at least across two targets. [3], [4]

Investigation note:
- Score 2 is appropriate because small molecule CNS penetration and oral delivery are a practical differentiation point against intrathecal ASO/siRNA competitors.
- Why not higher: the platform is not yet supported by multiple independent clinical validations or a broad disclosed engine; platform reproducibility remains partly company-claim based.
- Uncertain points: no public head-to-head data versus ASO/di-siRNA delivery approaches; long-term KCNT1 inhibition safety margin in developing pediatric brain remains to be established.

### 4.5 Expansion Potential
Score: **2 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E1_company_claim_or_scientific_rationale_only**  
Main line: **There is credible expansion logic into other genetic epilepsies, but public asset-specific expansion data are not yet sufficient for a 3.**

What was checked:
- Expansion beyond main indication
- Same biology/platform reuse
- Adjacent indications
- Multiple assets from same platform

Evidence trail:
- Actio’s Series B release states ABS-1230 is intended for KCNT1-related epilepsy and other genetic epilepsies. [3]
- Actio’s program page lists “Other genetic epilepsies” in IND-enabling for ABS-1230. [4]
- Actio’s platform logic explicitly seeks expansion from rare Mendelian diseases into more prevalent indications sharing underlying biology. [5]

Investigation note:
- Score 2 reflects plausible but still unvalidated expansion.
- Why not higher: expansion evidence is mostly pipeline positioning and scientific rationale; no public patient or animal data in non-KCNT1 genetic epilepsies was identified during this review.
- Uncertain points: whether KCNT1 inhibition is therapeutically useful outside KCNT1-positive epilepsy is not yet established; expansion could require a different causal mechanism or patient-selection strategy.

### 4.6 Data Maturity
Score: **2 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E4_asset_specific_clinical_evidence**  
Main line: **ABS-1230 has strong preclinical package and Phase 1a tolerability disclosure, and is now in Phase 1b/2, but lacks patient efficacy readout.**

What was checked:
- In vitro data
- In vivo data
- Quantitative result
- Reproducibility
- IND-enabling / GLP tox / PK/PD / CMC / human data availability

Evidence trail:
- IND clearance and FDA Fast Track were announced in July 2025. [12]
- First participant dosed in Phase 1a healthy volunteer trial was announced in September 2025; the trial was randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled and evaluated SAD/MAD, food effect, safety, tolerability, and PK. [7]
- May 2026 KYRON initiation release states Phase 1a doses, including multiple 20 mg doses, were well tolerated with no serious adverse events reported. [8]
- KYRON Phase 1b/2 is designed to evaluate safety, tolerability, PK, seizure activity, and neurodevelopmental outcomes in children and young adults aged one month through 21 years. [8], [11]
- AES poster includes in vitro potency, selectivity, brain penetration, and seizure reduction in KCNT1 mouse model. [9]

Investigation note:
- Score 2 is driven by active clinical entry plus Phase 1a safety disclosure and robust preclinical evidence.
- Why not higher: there is no public patient efficacy, no dose-response seizure reduction in patients, and no long-term pediatric safety data yet.
- Uncertain points: detailed Phase 1a PK/exposure, dose-proportionality, food effect, and CNS exposure bridging are not fully public.

### 4.7 Marketability
Score: **1 / 3**  
Evidence Type: **E2_indirect_or_class_level_evidence**  
Main line: **A. TAP ≈ 800 U.S. targetable patients; B. Unrisked Peak Sales ≈ USD 107.5M; C. Obtainable Peak Sales ≈ USD 100.0M; orphan pricing helps but KCNT1 is too ultra-rare to reach USD 1B without major expansion.**

What was checked:
- Targetable addressable patient
- Unrisked peak sales
- Competition haircut
- Pricing power adjustment
- Expansion capacity adjustment

Worksheet:

| Step | What to fill | Evidence / assumption |
|---|---|---|
| A. TAP | Total Patient Pool x Diagnosis Rate x Eligibility Rate x Treatable Subgroup Rate = 2,500 x 65% x 70% x 70% = **796**, rounded to **~800 U.S. targetable patients** | Actio estimates U.S. prevalence of approximately 2,500 individuals; KCNT1 Foundation says prevalence is unknown and underdiagnosis remains material. Diagnosis / eligibility / treatable rates are analyst assumptions requiring validation. [3], [17] |
| B. Unrisked Peak Sales | TAP x Annual Net Price x Peak Penetration x Treatment Duration Factor = 796 x USD 300,000 x 45% x 1.0 / 1,000,000 = **USD 107.5M** | Annual net price assumes a rare CNS launch price around USD 300k, informed by ICER 2025 median annual net price for newly launched drugs and rare epilepsy analogs; actual ABS-1230 price is unknown. [20], [21], [22] |
| Entry-order matrix | 3-player example: 1st ~50%, 2nd ~30%, 3rd ~20%; ABS-1230 could be first oral targeted KCNT1, but S230815 and ATL-201 may compete. | Same-target competitor count from Servier and Atalanta sources. [13], [15], [16] |
| C. Obtainable Peak Sales | Unrisked Peak Sales x Competition Haircut x Pricing Power Adjustment x Expansion Capacity Adjustment = 107.5 x 0.65 x 1.10 x 1.30 = **USD 100.0M** | Competition haircut reflects S230815 / ATL-201; pricing power reflects targeted orphan drug; expansion capacity reflects other genetic epilepsy optionality but no clinical validation yet. |
| Final score basis | **1 / 3** because obtainable peak sales are far below USD 1,000M. | Marketability is constrained by ultra-rare TAP despite high unmet need and likely orphan pricing. |

Investigation note:
- Marketability is based on obtainable peak sales, not rNPV.
- This is a U.S.-anchored model because the most explicit population estimate found was U.S. prevalence from Actio; global expansion could increase the figure but remains source-uncertain.
- Why not higher: even with strong pricing and expansion multipliers, obtainable peak sales remain materially below USD 1B.
- Uncertain points: true diagnosed prevalence, treatment duration, payer-accepted price, geographic expansion, and broader genetic epilepsy label are not yet established.

## 6) Final Take

One-line summary: **ABS-1230 is a high-quality science-first CNS precision medicine asset with strong target/MoA fit and oral small-molecule differentiation, but its commercial case is constrained by ultra-rare KCNT1 prevalence unless broader genetic epilepsy expansion becomes clinically credible.**

Recommendation:
- **Shortlist**

Most important diligence question:
- **Can ABS-1230 demonstrate clinically meaningful seizure reduction and neurodevelopmental benefit in KCNT1-related epilepsy patients while maintaining a safe exposure margin in infants and young children?**
